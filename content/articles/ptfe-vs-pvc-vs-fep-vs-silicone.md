# PTFE vs PVC vs FEP vs Silicone Wire Insulation: A Complete Comparison Guide

Choosing the right insulation is the single most important decision in specifying a wire. The conductor carries the current, but it is the insulation that decides whether a circuit survives heat, chemical attack, vibration and decades of service — or fails in the field. This guide compares the four insulation materials engineers specify most often: **PTFE (Teflon), PVC, FEP and silicone** — across temperature, electrical performance, chemical resistance, mechanical behaviour and cost, so you can match the material to the environment.

## PTFE (polytetrafluoroethylene / "Teflon")

PTFE is the benchmark for demanding environments. It offers an exceptionally wide service range — roughly **−65 °C to +260 °C** for nickel-plated constructions and to about +200 °C for silver-plated copper — and it is **chemically inert** to virtually all acids, bases, solvents, oils and fuels. Electrically it has a low dielectric constant and low dissipation factor that stay stable across frequency and temperature, which is why it dominates **high-frequency and RF wiring**.

PTFE wire is typically **tape-wrapped** rather than extruded, giving a uniform wall thickness and excellent cut-through resistance. It is self-extinguishing and non-toxic in fire, immune to fungus, UV and water absorption, and resistant to soldering-iron damage. The trade-offs are higher cost and lower flexibility than PVC or silicone. PTFE is the standard for aerospace, defence, atomic-energy, satellite and instrumentation wiring — anywhere reliability is non-negotiable. ## FEP (fluorinated ethylene propylene)

FEP is a close cousin of PTFE in the fluoropolymer family and shares most of its electrical and chemical virtues — outstanding chemical resistance, low dielectric loss, self-extinguishing behaviour — with a continuous rating around **−65 °C to +200 °C**. The practical difference is processing: FEP is **melt-extruded**, which makes it well suited to high-speed production, thin uniform walls, and compact, tightly routed wiring. It is also highly transparent and offers excellent low-temperature toughness.

Choose FEP when you want PTFE-class chemical and electrical performance with extruded processing and slightly lower continuous temperature, often at competitive cost for high-volume runs. (See our [FEP extruded wire](/products/fep-wires).)

## Silicone

Silicone insulation is the **flexibility champion**. It stays soft and pliable across a wide temperature band (roughly −60 to +200 °C), handles high voltage well, and is a favourite for applications that flex repeatedly — high-voltage EV harnesses, robotics, and appliance leads. Its weakness is **mechanical**: silicone has poor tear and abrasion strength and cuts easily, so it often needs a protective sleeve or braid in rough environments. Pick silicone when repeated bending and high heat matter more than ruggedness.

## PVC (polyvinyl chloride)

PVC is the industry default for general-purpose wiring because it is **inexpensive, flexible and easy to terminate**. The catch is performance under stress. Standard PVC is rated only to about **70 °C** (flame-retardant low-smoke "FRLS" grades reach ~105 °C). Under electrical overload it softens, can ignite, and — critically — emits dense, toxic and corrosive fumes. It also becomes brittle in sub-zero temperatures and degrades under aggressive chemicals. PVC is the right choice for cost-sensitive, benign environments (consumer electronics, low-voltage building wiring) — and the wrong choice anywhere fire safety, heat or chemicals are in play.

## How to choose: a simple decision path

1. **Will it see heat, fire risk, chemicals or RF?** If yes, you are in fluoropolymer territory (PTFE or FEP), not PVC.
2. **PTFE or FEP?** Need the highest continuous temperature (to 260 °C), tape-wrapped uniformity and maximum reliability → **PTFE**. Need extruded thin walls, compact routing and high-volume economy at ≤200 °C → **FEP**.
3. **Does it flex constantly at high heat, with protection from abrasion?** → **silicone**.
4. **Benign, cost-driven, low-temperature?** → **PVC**, ideally an FRLS grade.

## Why it matters for safety

A large share of building and equipment fires start at faulty, overloaded wiring. PVC insulation succumbs to overload and feeds the fire while releasing toxic fumes. PTFE and FEP do not propagate flame and are non-toxic — which is precisely why military and aerospace systems specify them, and why upgrading critical circuits to PTFE/Teflon is a permanent fire-safety improvement, not just a performance one.

### FAQ
**Is Teflon the same as PTFE?** Yes — Teflon is DuPont's brand name for PTFE.
**Is PTFE better than PVC?** For heat, chemical and fire performance, decisively yes; PVC only wins on cost and flexibility in benign environments.
**What's the difference between PTFE and FEP?** Similar properties; PTFE is tape-wrapped and rated higher (to ~260 °C), FEP is melt-extruded and rated to ~200 °C.
**Which insulation is best for high temperature?** PTFE for the highest continuous temperatures; FEP and silicone for ~200 °C with different processing/flexibility trade-offs.
