# How to Select PTFE Hook-Up Wire: AWG, Conductor, Voltage & Temperature Guide

Selecting PTFE hook-up wire comes down to five decisions: **gauge (AWG), stranding, conductor plating, voltage class, and colour**. Get them right and the wire will outlast the equipment it's in. This guide walks through each, with a current-rating reference table, so you can build a complete order from a real requirement.

## Step 1 — Pick the gauge (AWG) from the current

Wire gauge is set by the current the wire must carry and the acceptable voltage drop over its run. AWG runs *backwards* — a **smaller AWG number is a thicker wire** carrying more current. Use the table below as a starting point for PTFE-insulated copper (verify against your derating, bundling and ambient conditions).

| AWG | Cross-section | Resistance (Ω/km) | Indicative current |
|---|---|---|---|
| 32 | 0.035 mm² | 567.6 | 0.7 A |
| 30 | 0.055 mm² | 330.4 | 1.0 A |
| 28 | 0.089 mm² | 204.1 | 1.5 A |
| 26 | 0.150 mm² | 126.0 | 3.0 A |
| 24 | 0.252 mm² | 79.7 | 4.0 A |
| 22 | 0.382 mm² | 49.5 | 7.3 A |
| 20 | 0.596 mm² | 30.2 | 11 A |
| 18 | 0.950 mm² | 19.5 | 16 A |
| 16 | 1.254 mm² | 14.8 | 22 A |
| 14 | 1.940 mm² | 9.8 | 32 A |
| 12 | 3.021 mm² | 5.93 | 41 A |
| 10 | 4.930 mm² | 3.8 | 55 A |
| 8 | 8.784 mm² | 2.15 | 75 A |
| 6 | 13.537 mm² | 1.37 | 100 A |

**20 vs 18 vs 16 AWG — a common crossroads:** 20 AWG suits signal and light loads (~11 A), 18 AWG steps up to ~16 A for moderate loads, and 16 AWG (~22 A) handles heavier power runs. When in doubt between two sizes, size up — the cost difference is small and the thermal/voltage-drop margin is worth it.

> A genuine advantage of PTFE: because the insulation tolerates heat that would destroy PVC, PTFE wire can often run hotter and therefore carry more current in the same gauge, and lets you pack **more circuits per conduit** thanks to its thin, tough wall.

## Step 2 — Choose stranding for flexibility

For a given AWG, more strands of finer wire means more flexibility and better flex-life — important where the harness moves, vibrates or bends tightly. Standard stranding counts are **1 (solid), 7, 19, 37 and 133**. Use solid or 7-strand for static internal wiring; 19/37/133 for flexing, robotics, or frequently re-worked assemblies.

## Step 3 — Select conductor plating

Plating sets the temperature ceiling and solderability:

- **Silver-plated copper (SPC):** best solderability and conductivity; rated to about **+200 °C**. The default for most electronics.
- **Nickel-plated copper (NPC):** higher temperature capability to about **+260 °C**; choose for the hottest zones (furnaces, engine bays, reactor areas).
- **Copper-clad steel / high-strength alloy:** where mechanical tension is a factor.

## Step 4 — Set the voltage class (ET / E / EE)

PTFE hook-up wire comes in three voltage classes defined by insulation wall:

- **Type ET — 250 V:** thin-wall, dense internal wiring where space is tight.
- **Type E — 600 V:** the general-purpose workhorse.
- **Type EE — 1,000 V:** higher-voltage or extra-margin applications.

Match the class to your maximum working voltage with comfortable headroom.

## Step 5 — Choose colour (and striping)

Colour aids identification and assembly. Standard solids are Black, Brown, Red, Orange, Yellow, Green, Blue, Violet, Grey and White, plus Pink and Natural — and stripe combinations are available for circuit coding. Plan your colour map before ordering to avoid rework.

## Don't forget the sleeving

Where you need extra insulation, abrasion protection or to bundle conductors, pair the wire with **PTFE sleeving** sized for a snug fit over the conductor — a loose sleeve can trap moisture and lower insulation resistance. See our [PTFE sleeving sizes](/products/ptfe-sleevings) from 0.3 mm to 30 mm bore.

## Build your complete order

Putting the five decisions together gives a clean specification, e.g.: *PTFE hook-up wire, MIL-W-16878E Type E, 20 AWG, 19/32 stranded, silver-plated copper, blue, 500 m.* Note that PTFE is typically supplied in standard coil/spool lengths with a minimum order per size and colour.

### FAQ
**What gauge wire do I need?** Match AWG to the current (see table) with margin for voltage drop, bundling and ambient heat; size up when unsure.
**Silver- or nickel-plated copper?** Silver to ~200 °C with the best solderability; nickel to ~260 °C for the hottest environments.
**What does Type E / EE / ET mean?** Voltage classes — 600 V, 1,000 V and 250 V respectively.
**Why choose PTFE over PVC for hook-up wire?** Far higher temperature, chemical and fire performance, thinner tough walls, and more circuits per conduit.
